\item

  the editor to orient the reader. These words are not in the source text.
  \end{editornote}
\item \textbf{Footnotes} marked ``\textit{Translator's note:}'' provide

\item \textbf{Japanese terms} appear in their original characters with
  romanization and English gloss on first use:
  e.g.\ \jt{中星}{ch\=uboshi}, ``Middle Star.''
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{On Authorship}

The manuscript attributes authorship to \jp{牛田権三郎} (Ushida
Gonzabur\=o) and credits \jp{鳴川猛之助} (Narukawa Takenosuke) as
editor and corrector. The text belongs to a genre of ``secret market
transmission texts'' (\jp{相場秘伝書}, \textit{s\=oba hidensho}) that
circulated among Edo-period trading houses. Many such texts were copied,
modified, and reattributed across generations; some circulate under the
name of the legendary Honma Munehisa (\jp{本間宗久}) of Sakata, the
figure most widely credited with the origins of candlestick charting.

The reader should understand this as a text from a \textit{tradition}
rather than a single author's original composition. Whether Ushida
Gonzabur\=o was a distinct historical figure, a pen name, or a later
compiler drawing on the same body of oral and written market knowledge
is uncertain from the manuscript alone. Throughout this translation,
``the source text'' or ``the text'' refers to the manuscript as received,
without prejudging the question of ultimate authorship.

\chapter*{Translator's Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Translator's Introduction}
\markboth{Translator's Introduction}{}

The \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku} (\jp{三猿金泉秘録}), or ``Three Monkeys
Gold Spring Secret Record,'' is one of the most celebrated texts in the
history of Japanese market speculation. It circulated as a closely guarded
family manuscript before being revised and published by Narukawa Takenosuke
in the mid-nineteenth century.\tn{The Kyoto University manuscript is dated
\jp{嘉永四年五月廿日} (20th day of the 5th month, Kaei 4), corresponding
to 1851. It identifies itself as a copy of the K\=omond\=o (\jp{好問堂})
edition, published at Igarashi-tsu (\jp{五十嵐津}). The text references
the Tenmei era (1781--1789) extensively for its price data.}

The title alludes to the proverb of the three monkeys---``see no evil, hear
no evil, speak no evil'' (\jp{見ざる、聞かざる、言わざる})---here reinterpreted
as a warning to the trader: do not be swayed by what you \textit{see} others
doing, what you \textit{hear} as rumor, or what you \textit{say} in haste.
Instead, observe the deeper patterns.

\section*{The Edo Rice Market}

During the Tokugawa period (1603--1868), rice was not merely a commodity but
the basis of the entire economy. Samurai stipends were paid in rice, domain
revenues were calculated in rice, and the great rice exchanges---above all
the D\=ojima Rice Exchange (\jp{堂島米会所}) in Osaka---functioned as
sophisticated futures markets where ``book rice'' (\jp{帳合米},
\textit{ch\=oaimai}) was traded as forward contracts.\tn{The D\=ojima Rice Exchange was established in 1697 as a
spot market for rice. Organized futures trading (\jp{帳合米},
\textit{ch\=oaimai}) was formally authorized by the shogunate in 1730,
making it one of the world's first organized futures markets.}

\section*{Structure of the Text}

The manuscript covers several interconnected domains:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Cosmological framework}: The market is understood through
  Yin-Yang (\jp{陰陽}) theory and the Neo-Confucian concept of the Supreme
  Ultimate (\jp{太極}).
\item \textbf{Cyclical analysis}: Multi-year cycles of favorable
  (\jp{順来}) and adverse (\jp{逢来}) conditions, with specific articles
  and provisions for each season.
\item \textbf{Weather forecasting}: Detailed correlations between the
  sexagenary cycle and weather---storms, floods, frost, tides, wind
  direction---as predictors of harvest outcomes.
\item \textbf{The Middle Star system}: A mean-reversion framework using a
  long-term average price level as the reference for all trading decisions.
\item \textbf{Position management}: Systematic staged entry and exit,
  with detailed numerical examples and tree diagrams.
\item \textbf{Trading psychology}: Teachings on patience, discipline, and
  the danger of following the crowd, organized around the Confucian virtues.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Preface: The Cosmology of the Market}

\begin{center}
\jp{三猿金泉録序}\\
\textit{Preface to the Three Monkeys Gold Spring Record}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

\lettrine[lines=2]{T}{he Supreme Ultimate}\tn{\jp{太極} (\textit{taikyoku}).
The foundational concept in Neo-Confucian cosmology, representing the
undifferentiated unity from which Yin and Yang emerge. The opening
deliberately echoes Zhou Dunyi's \textit{Taijitu shuo} (Explanation of the
Diagram of the Supreme Ultimate, mid-11th century), grounding market theory
in the highest philosophical authority available.}
moves and gives birth to Yang; when still, it gives birth to Yin.
The principle of one stillness and one movement---Heaven and Earth rise and
fall according to this logic. Movement reaches its extreme and becomes
stillness; stillness reaches its extreme and becomes movement again.
Movement and stillness are the root of one another.

This is the principle by which strong qi\tn{\jp{強気} (\textit{tsuyoki}),
literally ``strong spirit.'' In market contexts, this corresponds to bullish
sentiment or upward momentum. Its opposite, \jp{弱気} (\textit{yowaki}),
corresponds to bearish sentiment.} and weak qi alternate.

When this principle is applied to all things, there is nothing it does not
encompass. When applied to rice, the qi of people---whether strong or
weak---is contained within this principle. Those who combine this
understanding with close observation of the market will find it reasonable.
In years of good harvests and times of fine weather, one should compare and
examine the rice markets.

This is the principle that transcends mere natural disposition.

\chapter{Favorable and Adverse Conditions; The Articles of Trading}

\begin{center}
\jp{順来・逢来} --- \textit{Junrai and Airai}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

The market moves in great cycles of favorable conditions (\jt{順来}{junrai})
and adverse conditions (\jt{逢来}{airai}). Within a period of roughly
twelve to thirteen years, favorable conditions persist for approximately ten
years, and adverse conditions for three.\tn{This roughly 10:3 ratio of
bull-to-bear years is a recurring theme. While the specific ratio is
debatable, the underlying insight---that markets spend more time rising than
falling, but that the falls are sharper---anticipates modern observations
about market asymmetry.}

\section{Favorable-Condition Years}

During favorable years, rice is generally at high levels. When old rice
(\jt{古米}{komai})\tn{``Old rice'' refers to rice from previous harvests,
as opposed to \jp{新米} (\textit{shinmai}), newly harvested rice. Old rice
was traded as a distinct commodity with its own price dynamics.} is in a year
of abundance, it will be cheap:

\begin{sourcetext}
Buy in the fourth month. By mid-sixth month, prices will be high---this is
where one sells. In autumn, buy again around the seventh month. Toward
year-end, the price of old rice will rise, and one should sell again.
\end{sourcetext}

This is the fundamental seasonal pattern of favorable years.

\section{Adverse-Condition Years}

In adverse years, the cheap period for old rice falls around the fourth
through sixth months. In autumn, one should buy, and by year-end the rice
price climbs. But the overall trajectory is downward---the trader must be
more cautious, take smaller positions, and be quicker to exit.

\section{Songs of the Seasons}

\begin{sourcetext}
In a year of favorable conditions, the autumn harvest is plentiful;\\
the highs and lows can be estimated across four seasons.\\[0.5em]
In spring, from around the fourth month onward,\\
buy at the fifth-month level;\\
by the end of the year, the high price will come.
\end{sourcetext}

In favorable-condition periods, the rice market's sentiment
(\jt{人気}{jinki})\tn{Literally ``people-qi''---the collective mood or
energy of market participants.} for each month can be read directly. The
fifth-month and year-end levels become the reference points. In a year of
great flood (\jt{大水}{\=omizu}), the rice market reacts through the
twelfth month.

\section{The Fifteen Articles of Trading}

\begin{center}
\jp{十五ヶ条} --- \textit{J\=ugo Kaj\=o}
\end{center}

Within the market, one must read the sentiment of the crowd---whether people
are inclined to buy or sell---and not act rashly.

\begin{sourcetext}
The large trader may think that because he has capital, he can afford to hold
a losing position. This is a grave error.
\end{sourcetext}

Even with great capital, if one buys recklessly and the market moves against
him, the losses compound. Conversely, a trader of modest means who reads the
market well and trades with discipline can accumulate wealth steadily.

The decision to buy or sell should not be made lightly. One should observe
for several days:

\begin{sourcetext}
When the rice price has been falling and reaches a point where it no longer
declines despite bad news---this is the moment to buy.\\[0.5em]
When rice has been rising and ceases to advance despite favorable
reports---this is the moment to sell.
\end{sourcetext}

Do not follow the crowd blindly. When all are buying, consider selling; when
all are selling, consider buying. The profit lies in going against common
sentiment, yet one must have sound reason for doing so.\tn{This contrarian
principle is perhaps the most famous teaching of the Edo-period market texts.
The concept predates the Western maxim ``buy when there is blood in the
streets'' (apocryphally attributed to Baron Rothschild, first documented in
print in the 1890s). The underlying trading ideas in this text likely
predate even this 1851 manuscript copy by decades.}

\section{The Thirty-Eight Provisions for Seasonal Trading}

\begin{center}
\jp{三十八ヶ条} --- \textit{Sanj\=uhachi Kaj\=o}
\end{center}

\begin{editornote}
The Fifteen Articles are followed in the source text by a more detailed set
of thirty-eight provisions governing month-by-month trading. These extend
the seasonal framework with specific rules for reading harvest signals.
\end{editornote}

In a year of adverse conditions, from around the fifth month onward, summer
rice drops and autumn months see further decline. Autumn months are for
buying; favorable months are for the pattern of waiting.

On the upper, middle, and lower rice-producing regions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Upper regions} (\jt{上作}{j\=osaku}): high yields; prices are
  depressed accordingly.
\item \textbf{Middle regions} (\jt{中作}{ch\=usaku}): moderate yields; prices
  fall between the upper and lower estimates.
\item \textbf{Lower regions} (\jt{下作}{gesaku}): poor harvest; prices rise.
\end{itemize}

For the months from the tenth onward, one should consider the first ten days
of each month. The rice market's movement can be read within each
four-to-five month period.

\begin{sourcetext}
In the fifth month, in a year of poor harvest, the rice situation becomes
tight; one should buy at the lower level. But in a strong-yield period of
four years, the rice pattern reverses.
\end{sourcetext}

When weak qi pervades the world and things seem to continue weakening, yet
the rice price is at a level that appears flat---this is the year of rice
buying.

\chapter{The Record of Patterns and Staged Entry}

\section{Record of Spike Patterns}

\begin{center}
\jp{穂足の録} --- \textit{Hosoku no Roku}
\end{center}

\begin{editornote}
The title \jp{穂足} literally means ``spike-foot'' or ``ear of grain,''
referring to the visual patterns of price movement. This section establishes
the principle of patient observation before committing capital.
\end{editornote}

When buying and holding, one must consider the fortune (\jt{運}{un}) and the
momentum (\jt{気}{ki}). The transport and supply dynamics, whether two
hundred days or three hundred days, the seasonal movements and annual
patterns must be compared.

\begin{sourcetext}
One should not be impatient: observe for two or three days, then when the
rice begins to move, buy. Those who buy impulsively and hold stubbornly will
fail. Gold and rice are like flowing water; one must read them well, buy
carefully, and sell at the right moment.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{The Tree Diagrams}

\begin{center}
\jp{萬光商内之圖}\\
\textit{Bank\=o Sh\=onai no Zu --- Diagram of Ten-Thousand-Light Trading}
\end{center}

\begin{editornote}
The source text includes tree diagrams illustrating the staged entry method.
These show how to build positions through numbered stages and how to
``revolve'' from buying to selling positions. The diagrams are reproduced
from the original manuscript below.
\end{editornote}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{diagrams/fig1_tree_positions.jpg}
\caption{Position-scaling tree diagrams. Right: building from a 1,000-ry\=o
base purchase (\jp{千十両買}) through four stages to a 500-ry\=o position
(\jp{五百両買}). Left: the 100-ry\=o sell diagram (\jp{百両売}) with title
cartouche \jp{萬光商内之圖}.
\textit{Image: Kyoto University Main Library.}}
\label{fig:tree1}
\end{figure}

The secret of profitable trading lies not in any single transaction but in
the method of building and unwinding positions through stages.

Starting from a base purchase, one builds the position through numbered
stages:

\begin{sourcetext}
Stage One (\jp{壱}): the initial commitment of capital---a small, probing
entry. This tests the market's direction.\\[0.3em]
Stage Two (\jp{弐}): when the direction is confirmed, a larger addition.\\[0.3em]
Stage Three (\jp{参}): further addition as confidence grows.\\[0.3em]
Stage Four (\jp{四}): the position reaches its full size.
\end{sourcetext}

The same principle applies in reverse when selling: one does not liquidate
the entire position at once but sells in stages, observing the market's
reaction at each step.

\section{Scaling to Larger Positions: The Turnover Method}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{diagrams/fig2_tree_scaling.jpg}
\caption{The scaling diagram: \jp{転愛商内之圖}
(\textit{Diagram of Revolving Trading}). A 1,000-ry\=o buying position
(\jp{千両買}) builds through four stages to a 2,000-ry\=o selling position
(\jp{二千両売}). \textit{Image: Kyoto University Main Library.}}
\label{fig:tree2}
\end{figure}

When buying positions reach their target, one begins to build selling
positions simultaneously---this is called the ``turnover method''
(\jt{転換の法}{tenkan no h\=o}).

\begin{sourcetext}
For every 1,000 ry\=o in buying positions, prepare to sell 2,000 ry\=o
worth. The reasoning: as the market reaches its peak, the momentum of the
crowd drives prices beyond fair value. At this point, the disciplined trader
sells more than he bought, profiting from the overshoot.
\end{sourcetext}

\begin{sourcetext}
When traders buy at low prices and see profits growing, they become greedy
and wish to hold longer. When they see losses, they become fearful and wish
to sell at once. Both impulses are the enemy of profit.\\[0.5em]
The disciplined trader sells when he wishes to hold, and holds when he
wishes to sell---going against his own emotions as well as those of the crowd.
\end{sourcetext}

This art was not to be shared lightly. It was passed down within the family
and among trusted associates only.

\chapter{The Sixty-Year Cycle: Weather, Tides, and Harvest}

\begin{center}
\jp{暴風 運気豊凶之録}\\
\textit{Record of Storms, Fortune-Qi, and Abundance or Famine}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

\begin{editornote}
This chapter presents the text's weather-forecasting apparatus---an
elaborate system correlating the Chinese sexagenary calendar with weather
patterns, harvest outcomes, and market cycles. Two hundred and thirty years
of records were consulted to build these correlations.
\end{editornote}

A twelve-year sub-cycle governs the alternation of fortune and misfortune in
harvests.\tn{The sexagenary cycle (\jp{干支}, \textit{kanshi} or
\textit{eto}) combines the ten Heavenly Stems and twelve Earthly Branches to
produce a sixty-year cycle. Each year has a unique two-character designation
(e.g., \jp{甲子}, \textit{kinoe-ne}). This system was used across East Asia
for calendrical and divinatory purposes.}

\section{Violent Storms}

In certain years of the cycle, violent storms and market surges are more
frequent:

\begin{quote}
\jp{甲子}, \jp{丁卯}, \jp{丁未}, \jp{甲戌}, \jp{甲申}, \jp{辛卯}, \jp{甲午}
\end{quote}

In these years, rice markets experience extreme volatility; prices may surge
two or three times within a single season. Traders must be especially
cautious.

\section{Floods}

Years containing \jp{己} (\textit{tsuchinoto}) in their stem are associated
with water disasters:

\begin{quote}
\jp{己巳}, \jp{己卯}, \jp{己丑}, \jp{己亥}, \jp{己酉}, \jp{己未}
\end{quote}

These bring three-year periods of disruption. Rice prices respond sharply.

\section{Frost and Cold}

Unusual cold damages crops and triggers three-year adverse periods. Rice
prices rise sharply in response.

\section{Bountiful Harvest Years}

Years of expected bountiful harvest (\jt{豊作}{h\=osaku}) were tracked across
twenty-eight years of records and correlated with the sexagenary years. In
autumn months, the yields were logged, producing a predictive framework
spanning the full sixty-year cycle.

\section{The Plum Rains}

The onset of the \textit{tsuyu} (\jp{梅雨}) rainy season follows an
approximately eight-year sub-cycle:

\begin{sourcetext}
Early plum rains generally indicate good harvests.\\
Late plum rains may indicate drought followed by flooding.
\end{sourcetext}

Associated years in the cycle:
\begin{quote}
\jp{丙寅}, \jp{丙子}, \jp{庚辰}, \jp{庚寅},
\jp{壬午}, \jp{壬辰}, \jp{丙申}, \jp{丙午}
\end{quote}

\section{Tidal Patterns}

\begin{center}
\jp{大潮小潮之差} --- \textit{\=Oshio Koshio no Sa}\\
\textit{The Difference Between Spring Tides and Neap Tides}
\end{center}

The tidal patterns of spring tides (\jt{大潮}{\=oshio}) and neap tides
(\jt{小潮}{koshio}) are analyzed in relation to weather prediction. In years
of great tidal difference, storms are more likely. The timing of tides in
relation to planting and harvesting seasons provides further indicators for
the harvest forecast.

\section{Wind Patterns}

\begin{center}
\jp{風} --- \textit{Kaze}
\end{center}

Wind direction and timing across the cycle:

\begin{quote}
\jp{戊申}, \jp{己卯}, \jp{丙辰}, \jp{丁巳}, \jp{庚申}
\end{quote}

In years of strong autumn wind, harvest damage increases. The period
between the seventh and ninth months is critical. Wind from certain
directions at certain times predicts the harvest outcome.

\begin{sourcetext}
A ten-year or fifteen-year span governs the wind's major directional shifts.
Across one hundred years of observation, these cycles hold with reasonable
reliability---roughly one year of error in every ten.
\end{sourcetext}

% CHAPTER 5: EIGHT POLES AND SIX OBSERVATIONS
% =============================================================================
\chapter{The Eight Poles, Celestial Signs, and the Six Observations}

\section{The Eight Poles}

\begin{center}
\jp{八極 三千目}\\
\textit{Hakkyoku Sanzenme --- Eight Poles, Three Thousand Points}
\end{center}

\begin{editornote}
The ``eight poles'' represent the outermost boundaries of market
behavior---the extremes beyond which a reversal is inevitable. The text
presents them alongside observations on crowd psychology and celestial
anomalies.
\end{editornote}

The categories of market force are classified:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \jt{陽}{Y\=o} --- Yang: the bullish, rising force.
\item \jt{陰}{In} --- Yin: the bearish, falling force.
\item \jt{太極}{Taikyoku} --- Supreme Ultimate: the equilibrium point.
\item \jt{陰合}{Ing\=o} --- Yin convergence: multiple bearish factors
  aligning simultaneously.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The General's Campaign}

\begin{sourcetext}
Just as a general must commit his forces decisively at the right moment, the
trader must commit capital when the signs align. Hesitation at the critical
moment means the opportunity passes.
\end{sourcetext}

\subsection{The Unanimity Signal}

\begin{sourcetext}
When ten thousand people are all of one mind---all buying or all
selling---the reversal is near.\\[0.3em]
A thousand people of the same opinion is a strong signal.\\
Ten thousand unanimous is an extreme.\\
At such moments, the solitary trader who goes against them has the advantage.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{Celestial Anomalies}

\begin{center}
\jp{天怪} --- \textit{Tenkai}
\end{center}

When extraordinary signs appear---unusual weather, disruptions to the normal
seasonal flow---the trader must first determine whether these are temporary
or lasting in their effect. The standard rice price across months, the
ordinary level (\jt{普通}{futsū}), is established over a year, and only
deviations from this level are traded.

\section{The Six Secret Observations}

\begin{center}
\jp{六秘見} --- \textit{Roku Hiken}
\end{center}

\begin{editornote}
The ``Six Observations'' provide a structured checklist for evaluating any
trading opportunity. They appear in the source text as a numbered framework
preceding a discussion of proportional entry.
\end{editornote}

When watching the rice market, one must first observe these six things:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \jt{二割}{ni-wari} --- \textbf{the 20\% level}: if rice falls 20\%
  from its high, this is a meaningful correction.
\item \jt{三割}{san-wari} --- \textbf{the 30\% level}: a major correction
  inviting careful buying.
\item \jt{高の内}{taka no uchi} --- \textbf{within the high range}:
  movements here are distribution, not accumulation.
\item \jt{横の平}{yoko no hira} --- \textbf{sideways movement}: the market
  is undecided; wait.
\item \jt{人の気}{hito no ki} --- \textbf{people's sentiment}: the crowd's
  mood as a contrary indicator.
\item \jt{大勢}{taisei} --- \textbf{the grand trend}: the overarching
  direction that governs all shorter movements.
\end{enumerate}

These six observations determine all trading. When a person wishes to buy,
he should first check whether the price has already risen 20--30\% from its
base. If it has, the opportunity for buying has passed; one must wait for
the next decline.

\begin{sourcetext}
All buying and selling should be done proportionally. When buying, one
should think of mountains---rice is like mountains and water; it naturally
rises and falls. The great mountain does not rise in a day, and neither does
the great price movement. One should buy in portions: first a small amount,
then add as the direction confirms.
\end{sourcetext}

\begin{sourcetext}
When a thousand people are of one mind regarding the market, and a single
trader perceives something different, he must weigh his own observation
against the crowd's. If his reasoning is sound and based on the six
observations, he may be right even against one thousand.
\end{sourcetext}

% CHAPTER 6: THE FOUR VIRTUES
% =============================================================================
\chapter{The Four Virtues of the Trader}

\begin{editornote}
The source text organizes its deepest teachings on trading conduct around
Confucian virtues. Four are treated explicitly: Benevolence, Wisdom,
Courage, and Discipline. The traditional Confucian system names five
constant virtues (\jp{五常}, \textit{goj\=o}), the fifth being
Trustworthiness (\jp{信}, \textit{shin}). Whether the fifth was omitted
deliberately, lost in transmission, or treated elsewhere in the tradition
under a different framing is unknown from this manuscript alone.
\end{editornote}

\section{Benevolence (\jp{仁}, \textit{Jin})}

\begin{sourcetext}
Buy once, and it returns to its original level. Wait.
\end{sourcetext}

When one buys and the price immediately returns to the purchase level, one
must not panic and sell at cost. The market is testing the trader's resolve.
This is the test of benevolence---patience and compassion for one's own
position.

If after buying, the price falls and then recovers to near the purchase
price, this pattern---repeated over three years---teaches the trader to
endure. In each of those years, the rice that was bought at what seemed a
bad time eventually proved profitable for those who held.

\subsection{On Upper, Middle, and Lower Positions}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Upper level} (\jt{上位}{j\=oi}): rice has risen strongly and
  is at the top of its range. \textit{One should be selling, not buying.}
\item \textbf{Middle level} (\jt{中位}{ch\=ui}): rice is at its ordinary
  seasonal level. \textit{Wait and observe.}
\item \textbf{Lower level} (\jt{下位}{gei}): rice has fallen to the bottom
  of its range. \textit{This is where buying begins.}
\end{itemize}

\begin{sourcetext}
When the price has been at a low level for three hundred days or more, and a
rise begins, and then the price returns and falls again to a new low---this
is the final bottom. Buy here.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{Wisdom (\jp{智}, \textit{Chi})}

Wisdom in trading encompasses knowing when to enter, knowing when to exit,
reading the mood of the market, understanding seasonal patterns, and
patience in waiting for the right moment.

The relationship between different regional markets---D\=ojima, Bakan, and
others---creates timing advantages. Information flows between them with
delay; the trader who understands this flow profits from the gap.

When favorable conditions turn and the market reverses, the signs are subtle
at first:

\begin{sourcetext}
The price may stop rising for several days; then a slight decline sets in.
Those who recognize this early transition profit the most.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{Courage (\jp{勇}, \textit{Y\=u})}

The courage to act when the signs align, even when the crowd moves in the
opposite direction. Courage without wisdom is recklessness; wisdom without
courage is paralysis.

\section{Discipline (\jp{節}, \textit{Setsu})}

Following the method even when emotions urge otherwise.

\begin{sourcetext}
The cautious man who holds stable reserves of twenty tawara and trades with
five will survive all storms. He who trades with his entire fortune will
eventually be ruined.
\end{sourcetext}

% CHAPTER 7: THREE LEVELS AND MIDDLE STAR

\chapter{The Three Levels and the Middle Star}

\begin{center}
\jp{世の中 三段之圖}\\
\textit{Yo no Naka Sandan no Zu --- Diagram of the Three Levels of the World}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

\begin{editornote}
This chapter contains the core analytical framework of the entire system:
the division of market prices into three levels, with the ``Middle Star''
as the central reference point.
\end{editornote}

\section{The Diagram}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.55\textwidth]{diagrams/fig3_three_levels.jpg}
\caption{The Three Levels Diagram (\jp{世の中三段之圖}). The circle is
divided into Upper (\jp{上}, 35 tawara), Middle (\jp{中}, 30 tawara plus
1 bu), and Lower (\jp{下}, 25 tawara). Below: the Ceiling (\jp{天井}),
Floor (\jp{底直}), and Middle Star (\jp{中星}) reference points with their
associated price tables. \textit{Image: Kyoto University Main Library.}}
\label{fig:threelevels}
\end{figure}

\section{The Three Levels}

The rice market at any moment exists within one of three ranges:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Upper} (\jp{上}): approximately 35 tawara level---the ceiling
  range where selling is indicated.
\item \textbf{Middle} (\jp{中}): approximately 30 tawara, 1 bu---the
  equilibrium range where one observes and waits.
\item \textbf{Lower} (\jp{下}): approximately 25 tawara level---the floor
  range where buying is indicated.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Middle Star}

The \jt{中星}{ch\=uboshi}, or ``Middle Star,'' is the central concept.
Across thirty years of observation, the average rice price establishes a
central equilibrium---the middle star.\tn{This is essentially a moving
average concept. The ``thirty years'' likely refers to the observational
dataset used. The principle---trading deviations from a long-term
mean---is the foundation of modern mean-reversion strategies.}

\begin{sourcetext}
When price is at the middle star: observe and wait.\\
When price falls to twenty percent below the middle star: begin buying.\\
When price rises to twenty percent above the middle star: begin selling.
\end{sourcetext}

In a year of abundant harvest, the direct price falls to the middle star or
below. One should buy at this point and hold, as in subsequent years the
price will revert.

\section{Position Calculations at the Middle Star}

The source text devotes several pages to detailed numerical examples of
position-building at the middle star level. These tables show how capital
compounds through staged entry at each confirmation point.

\subsection{Trading with 100 Ry\=o}

\begin{center}
\jp{百両売 天之商内} --- \textit{Selling with 100 Ry\=o, Within Heaven's Trading}
\end{center}

Starting from a base of 100 ry\=o at the middle star (\jp{中星世俵}) level:

\begin{sourcetext}
Stage One (\jp{壱}): 100 ry\=o committed, purchasing 20 tawara at the
middle star level. This is the probing entry.\\[0.3em]
Stage Two (\jp{弐}): A further 100 ry\=o committed as direction
confirms---position now 200 ry\=o, approximately 20 tawara held. At a
10\% gain (\jp{一割}), the profit is reinvested.\\[0.3em]
Stage Three (\jp{参}): Position built to 300 ry\=o. At the middle star,
this commands an expanded tawara position. Compounding accelerates.\\[0.3em]
Stage Four (\jp{四}): Full position reached at 400 ry\=o capital committed.
Through compounding of profits at each stage, the effective exposure has
grown to approximately 800 ry\=o---doubling the original capital through
disciplined staged entry.
\end{sourcetext}

\subsection{Scaling to 1,000 Ry\=o}

The text extends the same method to larger capital bases, with the
proportional discipline becoming even more critical:

\begin{sourcetext}
Stage One (\jp{壱}): 1,000 ry\=o over half a year (\jp{半年千両}).
First entry at 10\% (\jp{一割})---committing only 100 ry\=o initially.\\[0.3em]
Stage Two (\jp{弐}): At 20\% confirmation (\jp{二割}), add 100 ry\=o.
Middle star level maintained. Running total through 14 tawara positions.\\[0.3em]
Stage Three (\jp{参}): At 20\%, further 300 ry\=o committed. Position
accumulates at the middle star level.\\[0.3em]
Stage Four (\jp{四}): At 20\%, the accumulated position reaches 14 tawara
at full commitment. Total effective position: approximately 800 ry\=o in
profit across the staged entries.\\[0.3em]
Stage Five (\jp{五}): At 30\% (\jp{三割}), the final stage---the full
cycle is complete.
\end{sourcetext}

\begin{editornote}
The source text continues with further calculation tables covering
intermediate capital levels (200, 300, 500 ry\=o) and varying numbers of
stages, all following the same proportional method. The key principle is
constant: never commit the full position at once, and reinvest profits at
each stage to compound returns. The original tables occupy approximately
six pages of the manuscript.
\end{editornote}

% CHAPTER 8: SEVEN FORTUNES AND FOLLOWING GOLD
% =============================================================================
\chapter{The Seven Fortunes Secret; Following Gold}

\section{The Seven Conditions}

\begin{center}
\jp{七福即宝秘密}\\
\textit{Shichifuku Sokuh\=o Himitsu}
\end{center}

\begin{editornote}
This section reveals what the text presents as its core secret: seven
conditions that, when simultaneously present, indicate a high-probability
trade.
\end{editornote}

The trader must observe:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item The \textbf{seasonal cycle} is favorable.
\item The price is \textbf{at or below the middle star}.
\item Market \textbf{sentiment is bearish}---the crowd is selling.
\item The \textbf{harvest forecast} supports the position.
\item The \textbf{momentum} of recent days confirms a reversal.
\item One's own \textbf{capital is sufficient} to hold through adversity.
\item The \textbf{fortune-qi} (\jt{運気}{unki}) of the year aligns.
\end{enumerate}

When all seven align, one buys with full conviction through the staged
method.

\begin{sourcetext}
This is the ``instant treasure'' (\jp{即宝})---not that wealth comes
instantly, but that the recognition of the opportunity must be instant and
decisive.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{On Patience and Money}

\begin{sourcetext}
There is a saying: ``The money that was thought lost returns after three
years.'' In trading, positions that seem like losses in the short term often
prove profitable in the long term---if the fundamental analysis was correct.
The error is not in the analysis but in the trader's inability to endure the
interim.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{Following Gold}

\begin{center}
\jp{随金 商内} --- \textit{Zuikin Sh\=onai --- Following Gold in Trading}
\end{center}

\begin{sourcetext}
One's trading should follow where capital naturally flows. Do not fight the
movement of gold---instead, observe where it concentrates and follow it there.
\end{sourcetext}

\begin{editornote}
The distinction here is subtle but important: ``following gold'' does not
mean following the crowd's \textit{opinion}. It means following the actual
flow of \textit{capital}. The crowd may say one thing while their money does
another.
\end{editornote}

Gold and goods move in opposite directions. When gold is plentiful and goods
scarce, prices rise. When goods are plentiful and gold scarce, prices fall.

\begin{sourcetext}
When one has entered a position and the market moves sideways for many days,
doing nothing, the impulse to act grows unbearable. This is the critical
test. The trader who exits from boredom or impatience will miss the eventual
move.
\end{sourcetext}

\chapter{The Grand Diagram}

\begin{center}
\jp{三割上下割之圖}\\
\textit{Sanwari J\=oge Wari no Zu --- Diagram of the Three-Part Upper and Lower Division}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

\begin{editornote}
This diagram is the visual synthesis of the entire trading system, combining
seasonal cycles, price levels, and the Yin-Yang framework into a single
reference chart. The source text calls it the \jp{中星圓珠秘象}

\end{editornote}

\section{How to Read the Diagram}

The upper half of the diagram represents the Yang months---spring and
summer, the period of growth and rising. The lower half represents the Yin
months---autumn and winter, harvest and falling.

At each month's position, the diagram shows: the expected tawara level, the
indicated trading action, and the relationship to the middle star.

\begin{sourcetext}
In the four directions---representing the four seasons---combine the
seasonal tendency with the harvest forecast. When buying in spring, confirm
the middle star, then enter at the ten-percent level.
\end{sourcetext}

% CHAPTER 10: STRONG AND WEAK QI
% =============================================================================
\chapter{Reading Strong and Weak Qi}

\section{The Beginning of Strong Qi}

\begin{sourcetext}
When strong qi first emerges, the signs are these: a ten-percent rise from
the bottom; people are still bearish; news is still negative; yet the price
has stopped falling and begins to creep upward.\\[0.5em]
This is the critical moment to begin buying.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{The Beginning of Weak Qi}

\begin{sourcetext}
A small decline from a sustained high. People are still bullish. Good news
continues. Yet the price stops rising and begins to soften.\\[0.5em]
This is when selling should begin.
\end{sourcetext}

Just as strong qi begins silently amid pessimism, weak qi begins silently
amid optimism. The symmetry is exact.

\section{The Great First Decline}

\begin{sourcetext}
The first drop is sharp and frightening. Panic selling follows. But the
first major drop is rarely the final bottom.\\[0.5em]
After the panic subsides, a temporary recovery occurs. Then the slower,
grinding decline continues to the true bottom.
\end{sourcetext}

This pattern---sharp drop, bounce, slow grind---repeats across all markets
and all eras.\tn{This three-phase decline pattern is remarkably similar to
Dow Theory's description of bear market stages, formulated by Charles Dow
roughly fifty years later, and to modern descriptions of market crash
anatomy.} Patience is required to wait for the true bottom rather than
buying the first panic.

\section{Wind and Rain}

\begin{sourcetext}
When a storm comes and the ship is at sea, one cannot change the wind. But
one can adjust the sails.\\[0.5em]
Similarly, when market conditions shift suddenly, one cannot change the
conditions, but one can adjust the position.
\end{sourcetext}

The four seasons each bring their own storms. The trader who anticipates
them is rarely caught off guard.

% CHAPTER 11: REGIONAL MARKETS AND FINAL TEACHINGS
% =============================================================================
\chapter{Regional Markets and Final Teachings}

\section{The Geography of Information}

Osaka's D\=ojima exchange is the central rice market; all other regional
prices reference it. Information flows outward from Osaka to the provinces,
and the gap between Osaka and provincial prices creates trading
opportunities.

\begin{sourcetext}
The trader in the provinces who knows the Osaka price has an advantage over
one who knows only local prices.
\end{sourcetext}

Eastern provinces and western provinces have different harvest cycles. When
eastern harvests are poor but western harvests are good, the price in Osaka
reflects the average. A trader who can read the signs in the fields before
the market knows has the greatest advantage.

\section{The Essence of the Teaching}

In all the world's markets, the rice market is the foundation. What has
been written in this record is the accumulated observation of
generations---the secret teachings of the house, refined and corrected.

\begin{sourcetext}
The market is not a place of chance but of pattern. Those who study the
patterns---of seasons, of cycles, of human sentiment---can trade with the
odds in their favor.\\[0.5em]
But even the most learned trader will sometimes be wrong, and the method of
staged entry and proportional trading exists precisely for this reason: to
survive being wrong while maximizing the benefit of being right.
\end{sourcetext}

\section{The Four Virtues, Restated}

\begin{sourcetext}
\jt{仁}{Jin} --- Benevolence: patience and compassion for one's own errors.\\
\jt{智}{Chi} --- Wisdom: study and observation before action.\\
\jt{勇}{Y\=u} --- Courage: acting decisively when the signs align.\\
\jt{節}{Setsu} --- Discipline: following the method even when emotions urge
otherwise.\\[0.5em]
The trader who embodies all four virtues will prosper across generations.\\
The trader who relies on any one alone will eventually fail.\\[0.5em]
This record should not be shared carelessly. It represents a
lifetime---indeed, many lifetimes---of observation, and its value lies not
merely in the rules but in the understanding behind them.\\[0.5em]
A rule without understanding is dangerous;\\
understanding without discipline is useless.\\[0.5em]
To the reader who has studied this record thoroughly:\\
may you trade with wisdom and prosper.
\end{sourcetext}

\chapter*{Glossary of Key Terms}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Glossary of Key Terms}
\markboth{Glossary of Key Terms}{}

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=2em, font=\normalfont\bfseries]

\item[\jp{逢来} (\textit{airai})]
Adverse conditions. A period of generally falling rice prices, typically
lasting roughly three years within a twelve-to-thirteen-year cycle.

\item[\jp{中星} (\textit{ch\=uboshi})]
Middle Star. The long-term average rice price level that serves as the
central reference point for all trading decisions.

\item[\jp{帳合米} (\textit{ch\=oaimai})]
``Book rice.'' Forward contracts or futures on rice traded at the D\=ojima
exchange, as opposed to physical rice.

\item[\jp{堂島} (\textit{D\=ojima})]
The D\=ojima Rice Exchange in Osaka, the central marketplace for rice
futures trading during the Edo period.

\item[\jp{人気} (\textit{jinki})]
``People-qi.'' Market sentiment; the collective mood of traders.

\item[\jp{順来} (\textit{junrai})]
Favorable conditions. A period of generally rising rice prices, typically
lasting roughly ten years.

\item[\jp{古米} (\textit{komai})]
Old rice. Rice from previous harvests, traded as a distinct commodity.

\item[\jp{両} (\textit{ry\=o})]
The gold coin unit of the Edo period; the standard unit of capital and
accounting.

\item[\jp{新米} (\textit{shinmai})]
New rice. Freshly harvested rice.

\item[\jp{太極} (\textit{taikyoku})]
Supreme Ultimate. The Neo-Confucian concept of the undifferentiated unity
from which Yin and Yang emerge; here used to represent market equilibrium.

\item[\jp{大勢} (\textit{taisei})]
The grand trend; the overarching market direction.

\item[\jp{俵} (\textit{tawara} / \textit{hy\=o})]
A bale of rice; the standard unit of measure for rice trading. Also used
as a unit of price quotation.

\item[\jp{天井} (\textit{tenj\=o})]
Ceiling. The top of the price range; the point beyond which prices are
unlikely to rise further.

\item[\jp{強気} (\textit{tsuyoki})]
Strong qi; bullish sentiment or upward momentum.

\item[\jp{運気} (\textit{unki})]
Fortune-qi. The prevailing momentum or fortune of a given period, combining
seasonal, cyclical, and weather-related factors.

\item[\jp{弱気} (\textit{yowaki})]
Weak qi; bearish sentiment or downward momentum.

\item[\jp{底} (\textit{soko})]
Bottom or floor. The lowest point of the price range.

\item[\jp{随金} (\textit{zuikin})]
``Following gold.'' The principle of tracking actual capital flows rather
than market opinion.

\end{description}
